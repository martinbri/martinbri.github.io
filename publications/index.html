title: Vortex Gust Mitigation Using Deep Reinforcement Learning

author: Brice Martin

date: 2024-11

publication_url: here

Abstract: How to leverage deep reinforcement learning to solve vortex gust mitigation problems? In this dissertation, we approach the gust mitigation problem as an optimal control problem, modeled as a Markov decision process. Deep reinforcement learning is particularly well suited to solving these problems: first, it allows us to derive control functions regardless of the complexity of the aerodynamic model; second, the control function adapts to the fluid’s response to the control. However, for optimal performance, the controller needs to know in advance the locations and intensities of the unmeasurable gust vortices. Without this information, its efficiency is decreased. To address this issue, we employ a vortex estimation technique based on Kalman filters, which estimates the gust vortices using data from onboard sensors. Additionally, the learned control functions provide valuable insights into the mechanisms of gust mitigation. We reveal them using force partitioning techniques. Overall, this dissertation presents data-driven solutions for the dual tasks of vortex gust detection and mitigation, with a particular focus on understanding the physical mechanisms involved in these mitigation strategies.

title: Vortex gust mitigation from onboard measurements using deep reinforcement learning

authors: Brice Martin, Thierry Jardin, Emmanuel Rachelson, Michael Bauerheim

date: 2024-11

publication_url: “To come”

Abstract: This paper proposes to solve the vortex gust mitigation problem on a 2D, thin flat plate using onboard measurements. The objective is to solve the discrete-time optimal P control problem of finding the pitch rate sequence that minimizes the lift perturbation. The controller is modeled as an artificial neural network and it is trained to minimize J using Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL). To be optimal, we show that the controller must take as inputs the locations and circulations of the gust vortices, but these quantities are not directly observable from the onboard sensors. We therefore propose to use a Kalman Particle Filter (KPF) to estimate the gust vortices online from the onboard measurements. The reconstructed input is then used by the controller to calculate the appropriate pitch rate. We evaluate the performance of this method for gusts composed of one to five vortices. Our results show that (i) controllers deployed with full knowledge of the vortices are able to mitigate efficiently the lift disturbance induced by the gusts, (ii) the KPF performs well in reconstructing gusts composed of less than three vortices, but shows more contrasted results in the reconstruction of gusts composed of more vortices, and (iii) adding a KPF to the controller recovers a significant part of the performance loss due to the unobservable gust vortices.

title: Empirical Evaluation of Robustness of Deep Reinforcement Learning for a pitch control problem

authors: Brice Martin, Thierry Jardin, Emmanuel Rachelson, Michael Bauerheim

date: 2022-03

Abstract: Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) is intrinsically well suited for the control of complex non-linear dynamical systems, as usually encountered in flight dynamics. In this paper, we study the control of a non-linear determin- istic pitch control problem for an unmanned aircraft. We aim to evaluate the variation in performance of a DRL controller due to incomplete coverage of the observation space or noisy actuators. Specifically, we show that a par- tial coverage of the flight domain during the training pro- cedure impacts the efficiency of the controller, but retains the desired stability properties for the system. In addition, we evaluate the robustness of the controller to unmodeled actuation noise. We show that under limited perturbation levels, the average efficiency of the controller remains un- affected. As a basis for comparison, we also evaluate per- formance gains over a linear quadratic regulator
